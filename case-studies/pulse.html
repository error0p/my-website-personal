UI/UX Design · 2024

Pulse Health

Redesigning onboarding and core flows for a health & wellness app — reducing early drop-off and rebuilding daily engagement through clearer information architecture and a more honest design language.

Client

Pulse Health (Startup)

Role

Lead Product Designer

Deliverables

UX Research · IA · UI Design · Prototype · Design System

2024

The Problem

Users were leaving before they arrived.

Pulse had a retention problem masquerading as an acquisition problem. When I joined the project, the team had doubled down on paid marketing — but the real issue was upstream. 62% of new users were abandoning the app before completing onboarding, and the ones who stayed weren't forming habits.

After three sessions of user interviews and a thorough audit of the existing flows, the picture clarified: the onboarding asked for too much, explained too little, and the core app experience fragmented what should have been a simple daily ritual into five disconnected sections. Users felt overwhelmed before they felt helped.

The brief became: reduce the cognitive load of getting started, and design a home experience that makes the right next action obvious every time someone opens the app.

Approach

Progressive disclosure over front-loaded friction.

The key strategic decision was to flip the onboarding model. Instead of capturing all personalisation data upfront (the standard approach in health apps), I designed a "show then ask" pattern — let the user experience the app's value in 90 seconds, then progressively collect context as it becomes relevant.

This required rebuilding the information architecture from scratch. The five-section tab bar was collapsed into a single contextual home screen that surfaced the right module based on time of day and the user's declared goals. Navigation became invisible until needed.

I ran three rounds of usability testing across the process — one on the existing flow (to establish baseline behaviour), one on a low-fidelity prototype of the new onboarding, and a final round on the complete redesign. Each round directly shaped what went into the next.

Key Decisions

Decision 01

Cut onboarding from 11 steps to 3.

The original flow collected everything: goals, health conditions, sleep patterns, dietary preferences. All before the user had seen a single screen of the actual product. We deferred everything non-essential to in-app moments where the context made the question feel natural rather than interrogative.

Decision 02

One home, not five tabs.

Tab navigation was replaced by a contextual home screen that changes based on time of day and the user's active goal. Morning shows the day's focus ritual. Evening shows reflection. This made the app feel like it understood the user rather than demanding they navigate it.

Decision 03

Replace metrics with moments.

The original dashboard led with numbers — streaks, completion percentages, calories. User research showed these created anxiety rather than motivation for a significant portion of the audience. The redesign led with the next action, surfacing data only after completion rather than before.

Decision 04

Design the pause, not just the push.

Most health apps optimise for engagement by making it hard to stop. We made the opposite choice — designing clear, satisfying stopping points that reinforced a sense of completion. The hypothesis: users who feel finished are more likely to return than users who feel they're always behind.

Outcome

Onboarding completion tripled. Habit formation followed.

The redesigned onboarding launched to a 15% sample of new users in a three-week A/B test. Completion rate moved from 38% to 81% — exceeding the team's target of 70%. Critically, day-7 retention (the metric most correlated with long-term habit formation) increased from 22% to 41%.

The contextual home screen showed particularly strong results among the audience who had previously churned fastest: users without a prior fitness habit. The "next action obvious" pattern removed the decision overhead that had been causing the steepest drop-off.

The project also produced a foundational design system — a component library and token set that brought the brand's visual language into the product for the first time, giving the engineering team a single source of truth going forward.
