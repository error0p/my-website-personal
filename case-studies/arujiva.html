Arujiva Brand Identity System

Brand Identity · 2024

Arujiva

Building a complete brand identity system from the ground up — logo mark, color system, typography, and a full brand guidelines document for a brand entering a credibility-first market.

The Brief

A brand that earns trust before it speaks.

Arujiva came in without a visual identity of any kind — no logo, no colors, no guidelines. The brief was open: create a brand that positions them as credible, modern, and distinct within a space where most competitors default to the same visual language.

The core challenge wasn't aesthetic. It was strategic: how do you create something that feels confident and established, without looking like it's trying too hard? The brand needed to work across digital touchpoints, print collateral, and social media — all from a single coherent system.

The primary color decision — the teal that became #14AEC7 — came early and anchored everything else. It's distinct without being aggressive, modern without being cold.

Approach

System-first, mark-second.

Most brand projects start with the logo. This one started with the system. Before drawing a single mark, I mapped out how the brand would need to behave: on a business card, on a social post, on a document header, on a building sign. That exercise defined the constraints — and the logo mark was designed to work within them.

Typography was set in Lato — a humanist sans-serif that has the clarity required for business communication while retaining warmth. The weight range (Light through Bold) gave the system enough flexibility to handle both editorial hierarchy and functional labelling without introducing a second typeface.

The final system was documented in a brand guidelines PDF covering: logo usage rules, clear space requirements, color values (HEX, RGB, CMYK, Pantone), type scale, and a set of do/don't examples for common misapplication scenarios.

Key Decisions

Decision 01

Teal as the only chromatic color.

Most brand palettes hedge with multiple accent colors. For Arujiva, restraint was the message. A single primary hue — the teal — reinforced by one darker shade and a neutral off-white. This made the brand feel more decided, more confident, and more consistent across media.

Decision 02

Lato for all typographic roles.

Single-typeface systems demand a font that can carry both display weight and body copy without becoming monotonous. Lato's humanist structure gives it personality at large sizes while staying highly legible at small. The Italic cuts added editorial flexibility when needed.

Decision 03

Mark designed to scale to a favicon.

The logo mark needed to hold at 16×16 pixels — the favicon size — without becoming an unreadable blur. This shaped the simplicity of the final form. The single bounded shape with a single internal element reduces to a recognisable silhouette even at minimal sizes.

Decision 04

Guidelines written for non-designers.

Brand guides fail when they're too technical for the people who have to use them. The Arujiva guidelines were written for the client's own team — people who would be creating social posts and presentations, not professional designers. Plain language, clear examples, obvious do/don't comparisons.

Outcome

A complete system, ready to deploy across every channel.

The final deliverable was a complete brand identity system: primary and secondary logo lockups, a standalone mark for small-format use, the full color system with print and digital values, a Lato-based type scale, and a 20-page brand guidelines document.

The client used the guidelines directly to brief their social media manager and web developer — both of whom had no prior design experience. The system's clarity meant the brand stayed consistent from day one without requiring ongoing creative oversight.

The teal identity has since been applied to business cards, letterheads, social media templates, and the company website — all maintaining the same visual language established in the original system.
